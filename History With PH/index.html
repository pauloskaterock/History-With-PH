History (from ancient Greek ἱστορία, transl.: historía, meaning "research", "knowledge arising from the investigation") is the science that studies the human being and its action in time and space concomitantly with the analysis of processes and events that occurred in the past. The term "History" can also mean the entire information from the past stored in all languages around the world, through historical records.

The word history has its origin in the investigations of Herodotus; in ancient Greek, the term "History" is Ἱστορίαι (Historiai). However, Thucydides will be the first to apply critical methods, such as crossing data and using several different sources. The historical study begins when human beings find the elements of their existence in the achievements of their ancestors. This study, from the European point of view, is divided into two major periods: Prehistory and History.

Historians use various sources of information to construct the succession of historical processes, such as, for example, writings, recordings, interviews (Oral History) and archaeological finds. Some approaches are more frequent in certain periods than in others and the study of History also ends up presenting customs and fads (the historian seeks, in the present, answers about the past, that is, he is influenced by the present).

The individual who studies and writes about history and is considered an authority in this field is called a historian.[2] Historians are concerned with continuous and methodical narrative, and also with narrative that can be discontinuous and subjective, as well as the research of past events related to the human being, and the study of events that occurred over time and also in space. While the term historian can be used to describe both professionals and amateurs in the field, it is often reserved for those who have earned an academic degree in the discipline.[3] Some historians, however, are recognized solely on merit in their training and experience in the field.[3] It became a professional occupation at the end of the 19th century.
